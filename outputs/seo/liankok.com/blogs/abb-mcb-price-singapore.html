What Is the Difference Between 6kA and 10kA MCBs — And Which Do You Actually Need?

Breaking capacity (kA) is the maximum fault current an MCB can safely interrupt before tripping and isolating the circuit. A 6kA MCB handles fault currents up to 6,000 amperes; a 10kA model handles up to 10,000A. For most Singapore HDB flats, 6kA is technically correct. Singapore's HDB electrical supply is 230V single-phase, and the prospective short-circuit current at a typical HDB distribution board is 3–6kA — within the 6kA MCB's rated capacity.

So why does 10kA exist, and why do contractors sometimes specify it? For commercial premises connected closer to the utility transformer, for large industrial boards, and for sub-distribution boards in multi-storey buildings, prospective fault levels are higher. A 10kA MCB provides headroom. Specifying 10kA where 6kA is sufficient adds cost for no protection benefit.

MCB Curve Types Explained: B, C, D — Which Is Right for Your Application?

The ABB S200 is available in five trip curve types — B, C, D, K, and Z — each designed for a different load characteristic (ABB, new.abb.com). The curve determines how quickly the MCB responds to overcurrent. Getting this wrong doesn't just mean nuisance tripping — it can mean genuinely delayed protection when a real fault occurs.

Curve B: Lighting and Resistive Loads

Curve B trips at 3–5× the rated current. It's the most sensitive of the standard curves, making it the right choice for lighting circuits and resistive heating loads where inrush current is minimal. In Singapore HDB flats, Curve B is specified for the dedicated lighting circuit breakers in the distribution board.

Curve C: The Singapore Standard for Power Circuits

Curve C trips at 5–10× the rated current. It tolerates moderate inrush currents — the brief surge when an appliance switches on — without nuisance tripping. It's the standard specification for HDB power socket circuits, air-conditioning sub-circuits, and general commercial power distribution. When a Singaporean LEW specifies "20A MCB for power sockets," they mean a 20A Curve C. [INTERNAL-LINK: correct MCB specification for HDB → how to choose MCB Singapore guide]

Curve D: Industrial and Motor Loads

Curve D trips at 10–20× rated current. It's designed for loads with heavy inrush — motors, transformers, welding equipment — where Curve C would trip on startup inrush even though no real fault has occurred. Specifying Curve D on a residential circuit is a mistake: the slower magnetic response means less immediate protection during a genuine short circuit.

A common mistake we see: Contractors sometimes specify Curve D MCBs on standard power circuits to prevent nuisance tripping from high-inrush appliances. This is the wrong fix. Curve D means slower magnetic protection in a genuine short-circuit event. The right solution is to check whether the circuit is undersized, not to substitute a slower-responding MCB.

ABB S200 vs S280 — Which Series Is Right for Singapore Projects?

The ABB S200 covers current ratings up to 63A with breaking capacities up to 25kA, making it the correct specification for the vast majority of Singapore residential and commercial applications. The S280 series extends to 80A and 125A for larger commercial main distribution boards and industrial sub-boards that exceed the S200's upper rating limit.

For HDB flats and standard commercial tenancies, the S200 is the correct and most economical choice. Specifying the S280 for a 32A HDB circuit is over-engineering with no safety benefit and a higher unit cost. Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to replace an MCB in my HDB flat?

The total cost has two components: the MCB unit (S$8–S$25 for a standard 1P residential MCB from an authorised source) plus a Licensed Electrical Worker's labour, typically S$80–S$150 for a straightforward HDB MCB replacement, including call-out. Singapore's Electricity Act requires all electrical installation work in residential premises to be carried out by a licensed LEW — it cannot be a DIY job (Energy Market Authority Singapore).

Can I replace my own MCB in Singapore?

No. Under Singapore's Electricity Act, all electrical works in residential premises — including replacing an MCB in your HDB distribution board — must be carried out by a Licensed Electrical Worker (LEW). Lian Kok can supply the correct ABB MCB; your LEW installs it. Attempting DIY electrical work in Singapore carries both legal and safety risks.

What ampere rating do I need for my HDB power sockets?

Standard Singapore HDB power socket circuits use 20A or 32A Curve C MCBs. Dedicated air-conditioning circuits typically use 20A or 32A. Lighting circuits use 6A or 10A Curve B. Your Licensed Electrical Worker will specify the correct rating based on your actual circuit design — do not assume a direct replacement of the existing MCB without confirming the specification is still appropriate.

Are ABB S200 MCBs compatible with all distribution board brands?

Yes. The ABB S200 series uses standard 35mm DIN rail mounting (IEC 60715) and is compatible with all DIN rail distribution boards regardless of brand — Legrand, Schneider, Hager, and others. They are not proprietary to ABB distribution boards. The only incompatibility to check is physical space: confirm your DB has sufficient DIN rail slots for the breaker pole count you're installing.
