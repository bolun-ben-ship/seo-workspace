### H2: What Is the Difference Between 6kA and 10kA MCBs — And Which Do You Need? (~280 words)
- **Answer-first opener**: Breaking capacity (kA) is the maximum fault current an MCB can safely interrupt before tripping. A 6kA MCB handles fault currents up to 6,000 amperes; a 10kA handles up to 10,000A. For most Singapore HDB flats, 6kA is sufficient. For commercial premises and industrial boards, 10kA is the standard specification.
- **Key points to cover**:
  - What "breaking capacity" actually means in practical terms
  - Singapore HDB single-phase supply (230V); typical prospective short-circuit current at HDB DB boards is 3–6kA — making 6kA technically correct for residential
  - Commercial and industrial: higher fault levels mean 10kA is required
  - Common misconception: higher kA does not mean "better" for simple HDB use — it means higher cost with no benefit

### H2: MCB Curve Types Explained: B, C, D — Which Is Right for Your Application? (~270 words)
- **Answer-first opener**: The trip curve determines how fast the MCB responds to overcurrent. Curve B trips at 3–5× rated current (lighting, resistive loads). Curve C at 5–10× (general power sockets, small motors). Curve D at 10–20× (heavy motor inrush, transformers, welding equipment).
- **Key points to cover**:
  - **H3: Which Curve for Singapore HDB Flats**
    - Curve C is the standard specification for HDB power socket circuits
    - Curve B for dedicated lighting circuits
    - Incorrect curve = either nuisance tripping (too sensitive) or delayed protection (too slow)
  - **H3: Curve Types for Commercial and Industrial**
    - Curve D for motor control panels, HVAC compressors, welding circuits
    - Curve K and Z for specialist applications (electronic equipment, SCR protection)
  - Common installation mistake: specifying Curve D where Curve C is correct (delayed protection, potential safety hazard)
- **Key statistic**: ABB S200 available in curves B, C, D, K, Z — five distinct trip characteristics for different load types (ABB specification)
