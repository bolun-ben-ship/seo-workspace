### H2: Authorised Distributor vs Reseller vs Parallel Importer — What's the Real Difference? (~300 words)
- **Answer-first opener**: Three types of sellers supply ABB products in Singapore. Only one — the Authorised Distributor — has a direct relationship with ABB that guarantees product authenticity and manufacturer warranty.
- **Key points to cover**:
  - **H3: Type 1 — ABB Authorised Distributor**
    - Direct contractual appointment from ABB
    - Products sourced directly from ABB's authorised supply chain
    - Full ABB manufacturer warranty
    - Access to ABB technical support
    - Price reflects genuine product with full backing
  - **H3: Type 2 — Reseller**
    - Purchases from an authorised distributor and re-sells
    - May carry some warranty (passed through from the authorised distributor)
    - Cannot provide direct ABB manufacturer warranty
    - No direct ABB relationship
  - **H3: Type 3 — Parallel Importer / Grey Market**
    - Sources products from outside ABB's official Singapore supply chain
    - Often ABB products from other markets (different voltage specs, different certification marks)
    - No ABB manufacturer warranty
    - Cannot confirm product authenticity
    - Typically 10–20% cheaper at point of sale
- **Key stat**: Singapore's parallel import market is legal for many consumer goods — but for safety-critical electrical products, buying outside the authorised supply chain voids warranty and may contravene Singapore's Electricity Act requirements for certified equipment

### H2: Why Does Buying From an Authorised Source Matter for Safety? (~280 words)
- **Answer-first opener**: For most consumer products, buying from a non-authorised source means losing warranty. For circuit protection devices, it means something more serious: a product that may fail to trip when a fault occurs — the exact failure mode MCBs exist to prevent.
- **Key points to cover**:
  - How counterfeit and grey market MCBs enter Singapore's market (parallel import from non-Singapore ABB markets, online marketplace listings, unverified wholesalers)
  - Documented failure modes of non-genuine circuit protection devices:
    - Failure to trip under fault current → sustained overcurrent → fire
    - Nuisance tripping at normal load → false fault detection, operational disruption
    - Products passing visual inspection but failing under thermal stress
  - SCDF fire statistics context: 304 electrical origin residential fires in Singapore in 2025
  - Singapore's regulatory context: EMA requires electrical equipment to be certified to approved standards — products outside the authorised supply chain may not carry valid Singapore Safety Mark certification

    - The only reliable verification is supply chain traceability — which authorised distributors provide
