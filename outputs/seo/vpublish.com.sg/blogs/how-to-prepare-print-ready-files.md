# How to Prepare Print-Ready Files: The Complete Checklist for Singapore Printing (2026)

Your design looks perfect on screen. Then the printed copies arrive and something's off — the colours are wrong, a white border appeared where there shouldn't be one, or text near the edge got trimmed. None of these are press errors. They're all file errors, and they're almost entirely preventable.

At Visual Publish, the most common reason a job gets delayed or reprinted is a file submitted without proper print-ready preparation. This checklist covers every step you need to take before you send your file to any commercial printer in Singapore.

## Step 1: Set Your Colour Mode to CMYK Before You Start

Commercial printing uses CMYK inks — Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black. Screen displays use RGB (Red, Green, Blue). The two colour spaces don't map directly. Colours that look vivid on screen — especially electric blues, bright greens, and neon tones — will shift noticeably when converted from RGB to CMYK at the press.

The fix is simple but must happen before you design, not after: set your document colour mode to CMYK in your design software before you place a single colour swatch. Converting RGB to CMYK at the export stage is a workaround that causes unpredictable colour shifts. Starting in CMYK gives you accurate on-screen colour prediction from day one.

**In Adobe Illustrator:** File > Document Color Mode > CMYK Color
**In Adobe InDesign:** File > Document Setup — ensure the colour profile is CMYK
**In Adobe Photoshop:** Image > Mode > CMYK Color (do this before creating artwork)

For Pantone or spot colours required for exact brand matching, specify PMS values within a CMYK workflow. [INTERNAL-LINK: need exact Pantone matching? → read our guide to offset vs digital printing]

## Step 2: Set Image Resolution to 300 DPI Minimum

Resolution determines print sharpness. The industry standard minimum for commercial print is 300 DPI (dots per inch) at the final printed size ([Oppaca](https://www.oppaca.com/), 2024). Images sourced from websites are typically 72–96 DPI — sharply inadequate for print, and the pixelation is immediately visible on the finished piece.

Key rules:
- **Photos and raster images:** 300 DPI minimum at the final print size. If you scale an image up in your layout, its effective DPI drops proportionally — a 300 DPI image scaled to 200% becomes 150 DPI.
- **Line art and logos:** 600–1,200 DPI, or better: use vector formats (AI, EPS, SVG) that scale without resolution loss.
- **Screen captures and web images:** Do not use. 72 DPI web images cannot be upscaled to print quality without visible degradation.

Check effective resolution in InDesign via the Links panel (select an image, check Effective PPI). In Illustrator, check Document Info > Linked Images.

<!-- [PERSONAL EXPERIENCE] -->
> **From our prepress desk:** The most common resolution issue we see isn't a 72 DPI image — it's a 300 DPI image placed at 200% scale, which halves its effective resolution to 150 DPI. Always check effective resolution at your placed size, not the image's native resolution.

## Step 3: Add 3mm Bleed on All Sides

Bleed is the extension of your background, colours, or images beyond the final trim edge of the document. When a print job is trimmed to size, the cutting blade doesn't land on a perfect hairline — there's always 1–2mm of variance. Without bleed, that variance reveals a white border where your design should reach the edge.

The standard bleed in Singapore (and globally for commercial print) is **3mm on all sides** ([Thysse](https://thysse.com/), 2024). Set this in your document setup before laying out your design — don't add it as an afterthought.

**What this means in practice:**
- A standard A4 document (210 × 297mm) needs to be set up as 216 × 303mm to accommodate 3mm bleed on all four sides
- Background colours, photos, and any design element that should touch the edge must extend to the bleed line
- Text and logos that must not be cut should sit inside the safe zone (Step 4)

**In InDesign:** File > Document Setup > Bleed — enter 3mm for all four sides
**In Illustrator:** File > Document Setup > Bleed — enter 3mm
**In Photoshop:** Add 3mm to each dimension when creating the document; mark the safe area with guides

---

## Step 4: Keep Live Elements Inside the 5mm Safe Zone

The safe zone (also called the live area or safety margin) is the inward boundary from the trim edge where all important content — text, logos, phone numbers, faces — must sit. Even with bleed correctly set, the trim still has 1–2mm of variance, so anything too close to the edge risks being cut.

The safe zone recommendation is **5mm from the trim edge on all sides**. This means:
- No text within 5mm of the trim edge
- No logos within 5mm of the trim edge
- No critical visual elements within 5mm of the trim edge

Background fills and decorative elements can extend past the trim edge into the bleed zone. Only live content needs the 5mm clearance.

Visually: your document has three zones from inside to outside — the safe zone (5mm from trim), the trim line, and the bleed area (3mm beyond trim). Everything important lives inside the safe zone. Everything decorative extends into the bleed area.

## Step 5: Embed All Fonts or Convert to Outlines

Fonts are separate files from your artwork. When you send a design file to a printer, they need either the font files themselves or the text converted so it no longer depends on separate font files. If a font isn't available at the printer's system, the software substitutes a different font — which changes your layout, spacing, and typography.

There are two solutions:

**Option A — Convert to outlines (recommended):**
In Illustrator: Select All > Type > Create Outlines. This converts every letter into a vector shape. The file no longer requires the font. Verify outlines look correct before saving — text can no longer be edited after this step.

**Option B — Embed fonts in a PDF export:**
When exporting to PDF using the PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 preset (Step 8), fonts are embedded automatically. You can confirm in Acrobat: File > Properties > Fonts — all fonts should show "Embedded" or "Embedded Subset".

Note: If you convert to outlines before export, the fonts list in the PDF will be empty (correct — there are no live fonts remaining). Either approach is acceptable; PDF embedding is generally safer because it preserves editability in your source file.

## Step 6: Set Black Text Correctly (Avoid Rich Black on Small Type)

Black is not just black in CMYK. There are two types:

**True black (100K):** C0 M0 Y0 K100. A single ink channel. Recommended for all body text, small captions, and fine lines. Crisp, no registration risk.

**Rich black (mixed):** Typically C60 M40 Y40 K100. Multiple ink channels combine to produce a deeper, denser black — good for large background areas and headlines above 24pt. Do not use on body text or fine lines: even 0.5mm misregistration between the four ink layers creates a visible blur on small type.

The common error is applying rich black globally, then wondering why 10pt body copy looks slightly fuzzy on offset. Check each black element and apply the right type based on its size and function.

**Quick rule:** True black for any text under 24pt. Rich black only for large filled areas and display headlines.

## Step 7: Verify Document Dimensions and Orientation

Wrong dimensions and orientation are less common than colour or resolution errors, but they cause complete reprints. A job sent as landscape that should be portrait, or an A5 file set up as A4, can't be partially salvaged — the entire run is wasted.

Before export, confirm:
- Document dimensions match the ordered product exactly (check the printer's specification sheet, not your memory)
- Orientation matches — portrait vs landscape, especially for folded items (brochures, leaflets, DL envelopes)
- For multi-page documents: page order is correct, and the document is not reversed for saddle-stitch binding
- Spot colours and special finishes (foil, UV, emboss) are on correctly labelled layers if required

For standard Singapore product sizes:
| Product | Dimensions (mm) | With 3mm bleed |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| A4 | 210 × 297 | 216 × 303 |
| A5 | 148 × 210 | 154 × 216 |
| DL | 99 × 210 | 105 × 216 |
| Name card | 90 × 54 | 96 × 60 |
| A3 | 297 × 420 | 303 × 426 |

## Step 8: Export as PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4

PDF is the standard submission format for commercial print. But not all PDFs are equal — a PDF exported from a browser or word processor is very different from a press-ready PDF.

The correct export standards for commercial printing are:

**PDF/X-1a:** Oldest and most widely supported standard. All colours must be CMYK or spot (no RGB, no Lab). All fonts must be embedded. No transparencies (all effects must be flattened). Best for jobs going to older RIP workflows or when the printer specifically requests X-1a.

**PDF/X-4:** Newer standard. Supports live transparencies and ICC colour management. Preferred for files with complex transparency effects (drop shadows, blending modes). Accepted by all modern digital and offset presses.

**Recommended export settings regardless of standard:**
- Colour: CMYK (no RGB objects remaining)
- Resolution: 300 DPI for images, 1,200 DPI for line art
- Crop marks: On, offset 3–5mm from trim edge
- Bleed: 3mm (confirm this is included in the export settings)
- Compression: ZIP (lossless) for images — do not use JPEG compression in the PDF
- Fonts: Embedded or converted to outlines before export

In InDesign: File > Export > Adobe PDF (Print) > select PDF/X-4 preset, verify bleed and marks settings
In Illustrator: File > Save As > Adobe PDF > select the correct preset
In Photoshop: File > Save As > Photoshop PDF > set colour space and include bleed manually

## The Print-Ready File Checklist (Quick Reference)

Before submitting any file to a commercial printer, verify each item:

- [ ] Colour mode: CMYK (not RGB)
- [ ] Image resolution: 300 DPI minimum at final print size
- [ ] Bleed: 3mm on all four sides
- [ ] Safe zone: All live content 5mm inside trim edge
- [ ] Fonts: Embedded in PDF or converted to outlines
- [ ] Black type: True black (100K) for body text; rich black only for large fills
- [ ] Dimensions: Correct product size including bleed
- [ ] Export format: PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 with crop marks included

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What DPI should print files be in Singapore?

Commercial printers in Singapore require a minimum of 300 DPI at the final print size for photographs and raster images ([Oppaca](https://www.oppaca.com/), 2024). Line art and logos should be 600–1,200 DPI or supplied as vector files (AI, EPS, SVG). Images from websites (typically 72–96 DPI) cannot be upscaled to print quality without visible pixelation.

### How much bleed do I need for Singapore printing?

The standard bleed for commercial printing in Singapore is 3mm on all four sides. This applies to offset printing, digital printing, and most specialty processes. For large-format printing, some printers require 5mm bleed — always confirm with your specific printer before setting up the file.

### What file format should I use for printing?

PDF is the standard format for all commercial print submissions. Export as PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 with all fonts embedded, colour mode set to CMYK, and 3mm bleed included. Both offset and digital presses accept these formats. Original working files (AI, INDD, PSD) may be accepted by some printers but are not recommended — exported PDFs ensure your settings are locked.

### Can I use Canva files for professional printing?

Canva files can be submitted to printers if exported as a high-resolution PDF with bleed. In Canva, enable "Crop marks and bleed" in the download settings and select PDF Print format. However, Canva works in RGB by default — verify your export is set to CMYK, or note that colour shifts may occur during conversion. For brand-critical colour accuracy, use professional design software set to CMYK from the start. [INTERNAL-LINK: need Pantone-accurate printing? → compare offset vs digital printing]

### Why did my printed colours look different from the screen?

Screen displays use RGB colour mode; commercial printing uses CMYK. These colour spaces have different gamuts — some bright RGB colours (electric blues, vivid greens, neon tones) have no exact CMYK equivalent and will print darker or less saturated. The fix is to design in CMYK from the beginning, not convert at export. Use a colour-calibrated monitor and CMYK soft-proofing in your design software to preview printed colours before submitting your file.
